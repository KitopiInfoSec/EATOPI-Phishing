S  ·  the sender

Read the address.
Slowly, letter by letter.

The name on an email is easy to fake. The address behind it is harder — so scammers get close instead, hoping you only glance.

Tap the address below
From — line under glass
KR
Kitopi Rewards
Look at the spelling: it reads kitopirwards, but the real word is rewards — they dropped the e. At a glance your brain auto-fills it as "rewards" and moves on. Slow down and read it one letter at a time, and the missing letter jumps out. One wrong letter is all it takes.
L  ·  the links

The button says one
thing. It does another.

The words on a link are just a label. The real destination hides underneath it.

Press & hold the button (or hover on a computer)
Call-to-action — x-ray
What you see Activate Credit →

Where it actually goes .
The button really points to prizewel.com — a website Kitopi doesn't own and has never used. Anything real lives on Kitopi's own systems, never a stranger's. The habit: before you click any link, hover over it on a computer (or press & hold on a phone) and read where it actually goes.
A  ·  the action

What does this email
want you to do?

Every scam needs you to take one action. Spot the one this email is really pushing.

Pick the answer you think is right
Right. There's no attachment — the shiny button does the work instead. Tap it and you land on a fake "Kitopi login" page built to steal your real password. The A in SLAM is the Action it wants: open a file, log in, or leave Kitopi's website. A real staff perk would never ask for your password on an outside page.
M  ·  the message

Read the words.
They're rushing you.

Scams push you to hurry so you stop thinking. Each line below is a trick — tap them to see how.

Tap both lines
Body copy — two tricks
tap "Activate by 30 June… expire if unused." ⚠ A fake deadline — rushing you to act before you think
tap "A small thank-you… AED 200 free credit." ⚠ Too good to be true — free money to lower your guard
A fake deadline, free money, and dates that don't even agree (June… then November?). Real companies don't rush you, and they don't get their own dates wrong. When an email pushes you to hurry, that pressure is your signal to slow down.
